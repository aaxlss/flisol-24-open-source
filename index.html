Freelancing

Self-employment where individuals offer their skills, expertise, and services to clients on a temporary or project basis, rather than being employed full-time by a single employer.

  • Flexibility
  • Variety of Work
  • Independence
  • Income Potential
  • Continuous Learning
  • Flexibility: Freelancers have the freedom to choose their own projects, clients, and work schedules. They can work from anywhere, allowing for greater flexibility and work-life balance
  • Variety of Work: Freelancers often work on a variety of projects for different clients, which can provide opportunities for learning new skills, gaining diverse experiences, and exploring different industries or niches.
  • Independence: Freelancers are self-employed and have control over how they manage their work, including setting their rates, defining their services, and managing their time and resources.
  • Income Potential: Freelancers have the potential to earn a higher income compared to traditional employment, especially if they specialize in high-demand skills or industries. However, income can vary depending on factors such as experience, expertise, market demand, and the ability to secure projects consistently.
  • Continuous Learning: Freelancers need to stay updated on industry trends, tools, and technologies to remain competitive and offer valuable services to their clients. Investing in ongoing learning and skill development is crucial for long-term success.

Requires self-discipline, resilience, and proactive management

License

Copyleft Licenses

  • GNU General Public License (GPL)
  • GNU Lesser General Public License (LGPL)
  • Mozilla Public License (MPL)
  • Affero GPL (AGPL)
  • Lesser General Public License (LGPL)
  • Eclipse Public License (EPL)
  • any derivative works, which are modified or extended versions of the original software, must also be distributed under a copyleft license
  • Copyleft licenses ensure that software remains open-source and freely modifiable even after it's been incorporated into larger projects
  • Think of it this way: Imagine a recipe (source code) being shared freely under an open-source license (users can cook and modify the recipe). A copyleft license would be like saying, if you modify the recipe and create a new dish (derivative work), you must share that new recipe under the same open-source license (so others can enjoy and modify your creation too).
  • GNU General Public License (GPL): One of the most popular copyleft licenses, used in projects like Linux
  • GNU Lesser General Public License (LGPL): A less restrictive copyleft license used for certain libraries
  • Affero GPL (AGPL): Similar to GPL, AGPL adds an important clause: if you modify the software and run it as a service over a network, you must make the modified source code available to users. It’s often used for web applications.
  • Lesser General Public License (LGPL): LGPL provides the same level of terms as AGPL and GPL. It also preserves copyright and license notifications. LGPL is commonly used for libraries and allows linking with proprietary software.

Permissive Licenses

  • MIT License
  • Apache License
  • BSD
  • ISC License
  • are a type of open-source license that grant users a high degree of freedom with minimal restrictions. In contrast to copyleft licenses,
  • don't require users to share modifications they make to the original software under the same open-source license.
  • Freedom to Use: Permissive licenses allow users to freely use the open-source software in any way they see fit, including for commercial purposes.
  • Modification Rights: Users are granted the right to modify the source code and create derivative works based on the original software.
  • Distribution Flexibility: Permissive licenses allow users to distribute the software, both modified and unmodified versions, under any license they choose. This means derivative works can be distributed under a permissive license, a copyleft license, or even a proprietary closed-source license.
  • Permissive licenses allow for easier integration of open-source components into commercial projects without complex licensing conflicts.
  • the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. : A very popular permissive license, used in projects like Node.js and React
  • Berkeley Source Distribution BSD 3-Clause "New" or "Revised" License: Another widely used permissive license, used in projects like FreeBSD and OpenSSH.
  • Apache License: A permissive license that allows for both commercial and non-commercial use, used in projects like Apache HTTP Server and Cassandra
